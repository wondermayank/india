History & Heritage

3,000+ years of dynasties, monuments, stories and timelines — all in one place

01

📜 History of Delhi

Delhi's recorded history spans over 3,000 years, making it one of the oldest continuously inhabited cities in the world. Ancient texts identify the region as Indraprastha, the legendary capital of the Pandavas in the Mahabharata era. Archaeological excavations at Purana Qila have unearthed pottery dating back to around 900 BCE, confirming human settlement long before recorded history.

Delhi served as the capital of the Tomara Rajputs in the 8th century CE, followed by the Chahamanas (Chauhans). In 1192, after the Second Battle of Tarain, Prithviraj Chauhan was defeated by Muhammad of Ghor, marking the beginning of Islamic rule in Delhi. The city then witnessed the rise and fall of seven major cities — each built over or near the previous — including Qila Rai Pithora, Siri, Tughlaqabad, Jahanpanah, Firozabad, Dinpanah/Purana Qila, and finally Shahjahanabad (Old Delhi). Post-1857 uprising, power shifted to the British, who built New Delhi between 1911–1931. Since Independence in 1947, Delhi has evolved into the National Capital Territory, a mega-metropolis of 32 million people.

02

🌆 Old Delhi vs New Delhi

Old Delhi (Shahjahanabad) was built by Mughal Emperor Shah Jahan between 1638–1648. It is characterised by narrow winding lanes called galis, ornate mosques, havelis, and the magnificent Red Fort. The neighbourhood of Chandni Chowk remains the commercial and cultural heart of Old Delhi — dense, chaotic, and deeply traditional.

New Delhi, designed by British architects Edwin Lutyens and Herbert Baker, was inaugurated in 1931. It features wide tree-lined boulevards, imposing colonial buildings (Rashtrapati Bhavan, Parliament House, India Gate) and a planned radial street layout. Connaught Place forms its commercial centre. The contrast between the two Delhis is stark — Old Delhi's unplanned organic growth versus New Delhi's imperial geometry and open spaces. Together they form the world's most fascinating urban duality.

03

🕌 Mughal Heritage

The Mughal Empire (1526–1857) left Delhi with some of South Asia's most breathtaking architecture. Emperor Babur founded the empire; his grandson Akbar expanded it; and Shah Jahan gave Delhi its most iconic identity. Key Mughal legacies include:

Red Fort (Lal Qila) — Shah Jahan's palace-fortress, completed 1648. Jama Masjid — India's largest mosque, built 1656, accommodating 25,000 worshippers. Humayun's Tomb — a UNESCO site and the architectural precursor to the Taj Mahal, built by Hamida Banu Begum in 1572. The Qutub Minar Complex, though pre-Mughal, also saw Mughal additions. Mughal influence permeates Delhi's food (biryani, kebabs, korma), language (Urdu vocabulary in Hindi), crafts (zardozi embroidery, bidriware), and music (classical ghazals and qawwali).

🏗️ British-era Architecture

When the British shifted India's capital from Calcutta to Delhi in 1911, they commissioned a grand new city. Architects Edwin Lutyens and Herbert Baker designed a fusion of Indo-Saracenic, Classical, and Baroque styles.

Rashtrapati Bhavan (formerly Viceroy's House), completed 1929, features a massive Mughal garden and a dome inspired by the Pantheon. Parliament House is a circular colonnaded structure representing democratic ideals. Secretariat Buildings (North and South Block) flank Rajpath (now Kartavya Path) with colonnaded facades. India Gate (1931) commemorates 90,000 Indian soldiers who died in WWI. Connaught Place — a Georgian-style circular commercial district — was designed by Robert Tor Russell. The Safdarjung Tomb (1754), though Mughal, also reflects late Mughal-British transition. Old colonial bungalows of Lutyens' Delhi still house embassies and government officers, maintaining their original character.

👑 Famous Rulers of Delhi

Delhi has been ruled by some of history's most remarkable leaders:

Prithviraj Chauhan (1178–1192) — Last Hindu king of Delhi; defeated by Muhammad Ghori at the Battle of Tarain. Qutb ud-Din Aibak (1206–1210) — Founded the Delhi Sultanate; built Qutub Minar. Iltutmish (1211–1236) — Consolidated the Sultanate; first to issue silver coins. Alauddin Khalji (1296–1316) — Repelled Mongol invasions; built Siri Fort and Alai Darwaza. Muhammad bin Tughlaq (1325–1351) — Known for controversial decisions including moving the capital to Daulatabad. Sher Shah Suri (1540–1545) — Briefly overthrew Mughals; built the Grand Trunk Road. Akbar (1556–1605) — Greatest Mughal; known for religious tolerance and administrative reforms. Shah Jahan (1628–1658) — Built Red Fort, Jama Masjid, and the Taj Mahal. Aurangzeb (1658–1707) — Last powerful Mughal; empire declined after him. Bahadur Shah Zafar (1837–1857) — Last Mughal emperor; exiled after the 1857 revolt.

🏰 Delhi Sultanate Guide

The Delhi Sultanate (1206–1526) comprised five successive dynasties that ruled northern India from Delhi for over 300 years:

Slave/Mamluk Dynasty (1206–1290) — Founded by Qutb ud-Din Aibak, a former slave. Built Qutub Minar, Quwwat-ul-Islam Mosque. Khalji Dynasty (1290–1320) — Alauddin Khalji conquered most of the subcontinent; introduced price-control markets; built Alai Darwaza and Siri Fort. Tughlaq Dynasty (1320–1414) — Ghiyasuddin Tughlaq built Tughlaqabad. Muhammad bin Tughlaq controversially shifted capital to Daulatabad. Firoz Shah Tughlaq built Firozabad (Firoz Shah Kotla). Sayyid Dynasty (1414–1451) — A weaker phase after Timur's invasion. Lodi Dynasty (1451–1526) — Afghan rulers; Ibrahim Lodi was defeated by Babur at Panipat, ending the Sultanate. Sites to visit: Qutub Complex, Tughlaqabad Fort, Firoz Shah Kotla, Siri Fort ruins, Alai Darwaza, Lodi Garden tombs.

🕯️ Stories of Shahjahanabad

Shahjahanabad — built by Emperor Shah Jahan between 1638 and 1648 — was once the most prosperous city in the world. The walled city, roughly 6.5 km in circumference with 14 gates (including Kashmere Gate, Delhi Gate, Ajmeri Gate), housed an estimated 4 lakh people.

The Chandni Chowk bazaar was designed by Shah Jahan's daughter Jahanara and flanked by a canal reflecting moonlight (hence "Chandni" — moonlit). The street hosted jewellers, silversmiths, spice merchants, and poets. The city's culture was profoundly Urdu — it was the birthplace of poets like Mirza Ghalib and Bahadur Shah Zafar. After the 1857 revolt, the British demolished much of the walled city to build railway lines and barracks. Yet the mohallas (neighbourhoods) of Ballimaran, Matia Mahal, Hauz Qazi, and Darya Ganj still breathe the spirit of Shahjahanabad. Walking through its lanes today is like stepping into a living museum.

🚶 Heritage Walks

Delhi's streets are outdoor museums. Several curated heritage walks take you through centuries of history on foot:

Chandni Chowk Walk — Start at Red Fort, walk past Jama Masjid, the spice market Khari Baoli, Gauri Shankar Temple, Fatehpuri Mosque, and Ballimaran (Ghalib's mohalla). Mehrauli Archaeological Park Walk — Over 80 monuments spanning 1,000 years in one park, including Jamali Kamali Mosque and Balban's Tomb. Nizamuddin Basti Walk — Sufi dargahs, old havelis, Humayun's Tomb vicinity, and Thursday qawwali evenings. Lodi Garden Walk — 15th-century Lodi and Sayyid tombs amid manicured lawns. Agrasen ki Baoli Walk — A 14th-century stepwell hidden between skyscrapers in Connaught Place. Organizations offering guided walks: INTACH Delhi, Delhi Heritage Walks, Gullywale Tours. Best time: October–March, early mornings.

🌍 UNESCO Heritage Places

Delhi is home to three UNESCO World Heritage Sites — a concentration rivalled by few cities globally:

Qutub Minar Complex (1993) — Built in the 12th–13th centuries, includes the 73-metre Qutb Minar (world's tallest brick minaret), Quwwat-ul-Islam Mosque (first mosque in India), Alai Darwaza, and the famous Iron Pillar of Chandragupta II (c. 375–415 CE) which has resisted corrosion for 1,600 years.

Humayun's Tomb (1993) — Built in 1572 by Hamida Banu Begum, this was the first garden-tomb (charbagh style) in the subcontinent and directly inspired the Taj Mahal. It shelters the remains of Humayun and over 100 Mughal family members.

Red Fort Complex (2007) — Shah Jahan's magnificent palace-fortress on the banks of the Yamuna. Its Diwan-i-Aam, Diwan-i-Khas, Rang Mahal, and Moti Masjid represent the pinnacle of Mughal artisanship. India's Prime Minister delivers the Independence Day address from its ramparts every August 15th.

🍽️ Street Food Guide

Delhi's street food culture is unparalleled in India. The city's 30+ million people have created a food ecosystem where every lane, corner, and market has its own legendary stall. Key street foods to try:

Gol Gappa / Pani Puri — Crispy hollow spheres filled with spiced water, tamarind chutney, and chickpeas. A Delhi staple found everywhere. Chaat — A category of sour-spicy-sweet snacks: aloo chaat, dahi bhalla, papdi chaat, bhel puri. Best at Bengali Market, Chandni Chowk, and Lajpat Nagar. Aloo Tikki — Spiced potato patties served with chutneys, popular since the 1950s. Kulcha Naan — Leavened flatbreads from tandoor ovens, best paired with chole. Momos — Tibetan dumplings, ubiquitous in South and North Campus areas, now a Delhi staple. Rolls and Wraps — Kathi rolls (originally Kolkata) adopted and evolved by Delhi street vendors. Best areas: Chandni Chowk, South Extension, Karol Bagh, Lajpat Nagar Market, Satya Niketan.

🛺 Chandni Chowk Food Tour

Chandni Chowk is Asia's oldest market street and arguably India's greatest food destination. A food tour here covers 400 years of culinary history.

Paranthe Wali Gali — A narrow lane with 3–4 legendary shops serving stuffed parathas since the 1870s. Try mooli (radish), gobhi (cauliflower), and keema parathas. Jalebi Wala (Old Famous Jalebi Wala) — Near Dariba Kalan, operating since 1884. Their crispy jalebis dripping with syrup are unmissable. Natraj Dahi Bhalle Wala — Their dahi bhalle (lentil dumplings in yogurt) have a 70-year legacy. Kuremal Mohan Lal Kulfi — Fruit-stuffed kulfi made since 1906 in Sitaram Bazaar. Al Jawahar Restaurant — Near Jama Masjid, known for authentic Mughlai cuisine since 1947. Giani's Di Hatti — Faluda ice cream and lassi near the Red Fort. Best visited on an empty stomach, on foot, in the morning.

🫘 Best Chole Bhature Spots

Chole Bhature — spiced chickpea curry with fried puffed bread — is arguably Delhi's most iconic dish. The dish has Punjabi origins and reached Delhi with the massive post-Partition migration.

Sita Ram Diwan Chand (Paharganj, est. 1950s) — Queues form by 8 AM. Their chole has a uniquely smoky, dark masala. Opens only until noon. Haldiram's — The national chain's Chandni Chowk outlet remains a benchmark. Nand di Hatti (Kamla Nagar) — Beloved by Delhi University students. Chache Di Hatti (Kamla Nagar) — Operated by brothers, their chole-bhature is a North Campus institution. Baba Nagpal Corner (Lajpat Nagar) — Evening chole-kulche specialist since the 1980s. Roshan Di Kulfi, Karol Bagh — Also known for their morning chole bhature. The best chole is distinguished by its masala depth, the texture of chickpeas, and the oil-soaked bhatura's chewiness.

🫓 Famous Paratha Places

The stuffed paratha is Delhi's ultimate comfort food — a thick whole-wheat flatbread filled with spiced vegetables, paneer, or meat, cooked on a tava with generous ghee or butter.

Paranthe Wali Gali, Chandni Chowk — Three competing shops (Kanhaiyalal, Premchand Paranthewale, Gaya Paranthewale) all claiming to be the original since 1875. Each offers 20+ stuffing varieties. The Paratha Factory (Multiple Outlets) — Modern take on the classic, popular in South Delhi. Moolchand Parantha (Moolchand Flyover) — A Delhi classic open late nights, popular with office workers. Eatopia (Hauz Khas) — Upscale parathas with creative stuffings. Kake Da Hotel (Connaught Place) — Old-school Punjabi thali including parathas. The ultimate paratha is served with white butter (makhan), fresh curd (dahi), and house-made pickle (achaar).

🫕 Mughlai Food Culture

Mughlai cuisine — the culinary legacy of the Mughal courts — is a fusion of Persian, Central Asian, and Indian cooking traditions refined over 300 years. It is characterised by rich gravies, slow-cooked meats, aromatic spices, dry fruits, and saffron.

Biryani — Layered rice and meat dish; Delhi's "Dilli-style" biryani (lighter than Hyderabadi) is found at Karim's, Al Jawahar, Jawahar. Kebabs — Seekh kebab (minced), shammi kebab (lentil-meat), galouti (melt-in-mouth patty from Lucknow style), and kakori kebab. Best at Karim's, near Jama Masjid (since 1913). Nihari — A slow-cooked meat stew traditionally eaten at dawn by Mughal court workers. Korma — Braised meat in yogurt-nut gravy. Shahi Tukda — Bread pudding with rabdi and rose syrup. Mughlai food in Delhi is best experienced in the lanes around Jama Masjid — especially at Aslam's Chicken, Al Jawahar, and Haji Noora.

Tourist Places

Iconic landmarks, hidden gems, museums, and photography spots across the capital

01

🕯️ India Gate Guide

India Gate (originally All India War Memorial) is a 42-metre tall triumphal arch standing at the eastern end of Kartavya Path (formerly Rajpath). Designed by Edwin Lutyens and completed in 1931, it commemorates over 90,000 Indian soldiers who died in World War I and the Third Anglo-Afghan War. The names of 13,300 soldiers are inscribed on its surface.

At its base burns the Amar Jawan Jyoti (eternal flame), inaugurated in 1972 by Prime Minister Indira Gandhi. In January 2022, the Amar Jawan Jyoti was merged with the National War Memorial flame nearby. India Gate is surrounded by a large circular lawn — a popular gathering spot for evening picnics. The area is illuminated beautifully at night. Republic Day parade passes through here every January 26. Best time to visit: Sunset to 10 PM. Entry: Free. Nearest Metro: Central Secretariat (Yellow/Violet line).
1931LutyensWWI MemorialFree Entry
02

🔴 Red Fort Guide

Red Fort (Lal Qila) — a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 2007 — was built by Emperor Shah Jahan as his palace-fortress in Shahjahanabad, completed in 1648. The fort is named for its massive red sandstone walls, which stretch nearly 2.5 km and rise 33 metres on the riverside.

Key attractions inside: Lahori Gate (main entrance), Chhatta Chowk (covered bazaar), Diwan-i-Aam (Hall of Public Audience), Diwan-i-Khas (Hall of Private Audience, once housing the Peacock Throne), Rang Mahal (Palace of Colours), Mumtaz Mahal (now a museum), Moti Masjid (Pearl Mosque). The fort also hosts a Sound & Light Show in the evenings narrated by Amitabh Bachchan. India's Prime Minister delivers the Independence Day speech from the fort's ramparts every August 15. Entry: ₹35 (Indians), ₹550 (foreigners). Open: Tuesday–Sunday, 9:30 AM–4:30 PM.
UNESCO 2007Shah Jahan1648Aug 15
03

🗼 Qutub Minar Guide

Qutub Minar is a 73-metre tall fluted minaret of marble and red sandstone — the world's tallest brick minaret. Construction began in 1193 under Qutb ud-Din Aibak (Delhi Sultanate's first sultan), completed by Iltutmish in 1220, and later repaired by Firoz Shah Tughlaq in the 14th century. It has five tapering storeys, with a 14.32-metre base diameter narrowing to 2.75 metres at the top.

The complex also includes: Quwwat-ul-Islam Mosque (1193, India's first mosque), Iron Pillar of Chandragupta II (c. 375–415 CE), Alai Darwaza (1311, built by Alauddin Khalji), Iltutmish's Tomb and Alai Minar (unfinished). The Iron Pillar, made of 98% pure iron, has not rusted in 1,600 years — a marvel of ancient metallurgy. Entry: ₹40 (Indians), ₹600 (foreigners). Open: Sunrise to Sunset, daily. UNESCO: 1993.

04

🏯 Humayun's Tomb Guide

Humayun's Tomb (UNESCO 1993) was built in 1572 by Humayun's first wife Hamida Banu Begum, commissioned 14 years after the emperor's death. Designed by Persian architect Mirak Mirza Ghiyas, it is the first garden-tomb in the Indian subcontinent and the direct architectural predecessor of the Taj Mahal.

Set within a Persian charbagh (four-part garden), the main tomb rises 47 metres, clad in red sandstone with white marble inlay and a double dome — a first in India. The complex shelters over 100 Mughal family members in its chambers, earning it the nickname "Dormitory of the Mughals." Highlights: Chillah Nizamuddin Aulia (nearby Sufi saint's retreat), Isa Khan's Tomb (an earlier Afghan noble's octagonal tomb), Barber's Tomb, and Bu Halima's Garden. Entry: ₹40 (Indians), ₹600 (foreigners). Open: Sunrise to Sunset daily. Avoid Mondays (maintenance).
UNESCO 19931572CharbaghTaj Inspiration
05

🌸 Lotus Temple Guide

The Lotus Temple is a Bahá'í House of Worship completed in 1986, designed by Iranian-Canadian architect Fariborz Sahba. Shaped like a blooming lotus flower with 27 marble-clad petals arranged in groups of three, it sits in the Bahapur village area of South Delhi and is one of the most visited buildings in the world — reportedly receiving over 10,000 visitors daily.

The structure is made of Greek Pentelic marble (the same used for the Parthenon). Nine pools surround the building, giving the impression the flower floats on water. Open to people of all faiths, there is no religious ceremony or service — it is a place of silent meditation. No photography inside the prayer hall. Entry: Free. Open: Tuesday–Sunday, 9 AM–5:30 PM (Oct–Mar), 9 AM–7 PM (Apr–Sep). Nearest Metro: Kalkaji Mandir (Pink/Violet line).

📸 Photography Spots

01

🏛️ Connaught Place Guide

Connaught Place (CP) — officially Rajiv Chowk — is Delhi's iconic circular commercial hub, designed by British architect Robert Tor Russell in the 1930s in Georgian/Regency style. Three concentric rings of colonnaded shops form the Inner, Middle, and Outer Circles, connected by radial roads.

CP hosts Delhi's grandest mix of colonial-era cafés (United Coffee House, 1942), international brands, government offices, multiplexes, and underground markets. Palika Bazaar — an underground shopping market offering electronics, clothing, and pirated goods. Janpath Market — Open-air bazaar with Tibetan craft items, handicrafts, and vintage. The famous REGAL Cinema building (closed but iconic). Notable restaurants: Kwality (1940s), Rodeo, Saravana Bhavan. The area's underground Rajiv Chowk Metro Station is India's busiest, serving Yellow and Blue lines. Best for: branded shopping, dining, late-night café culture, people-watching.

02

🕌 Chandni Chowk Guide

Chandni Chowk — built 1650 by Jahanara Begum — is one of South Asia's oldest and busiest markets. The 1.5-km main street has been divided into specialty bazaars for centuries: Khari Baoli (spices, Asia's largest wholesale spice market), Kinari Bazaar (wedding accessories, zari, and tinsel), Dariba Kalan (silver and gold jewellery since Mughal times), Chawri Bazaar (paper, stationery, wedding cards), Nai Sarak (books and textbooks), Fatehpuri Mosque end (dry fruits and spices).

Recent revamp (2018–2021) added a pedestrianised stretch from Red Fort to Fatehpuri Mosque. Major landmarks: Red Fort, Jama Masjid, Gauri Shankar Temple, Sis Ganj Gurudwara (a Sikh shrine marking Guru Teg Bahadur's martyrdom). Best time: Weekday mornings 9 AM–12 PM before crowds peak.

🏡 Hauz Khas Guide

Hauz Khas is Delhi's most eclectic neighbourhood — a medieval complex of a 13th-century reservoir, Feroz Shah Tughlaq's madrasa and tomb, and a deer park, surrounded by one of Delhi's trendiest urban villages.

Hauz Khas Village (HKV) — Narrow lanes of boutique fashion shops, art galleries, design studios, rooftop cafés, and bars carved into and around the ancient structures. The contrast of Tughlaq-era ruins and modern nightlife is surreal. Hauz Khas Fort & Lake — Built 1352; the fort and madrasa overlook the reservoir (hauz = tank, khas = royal). Deer Park — Peaceful 70-acre park with spotted deer, blackbuck, and peacocks; ideal for morning walks. Shopping: Independent designer labels, handloom fabric, indie jewellery, and global streetwear. Eating: Social (rooftop bar with panoramic views), Yeti (Himalayan food), Naivedyam (South Indian). Metro: Yellow Line, Hauz Khas Station.

🔹 The Iron Pillar in the Qutub Complex has not rusted in 1,600 years; scientists still debate why.
🔹 Rajiv Chowk Metro Station is one of the world's busiest underground stations by footfall.
🔹 Delhi has more trees than human residents in some Lutyens zones due to a colonial planning mandate.
🔹 The Yamuna floodplain in Delhi covers 97 sq km — mostly unused — making it one of the world's largest urban floodplains.
🔹 Delhi's air quality in winter (Nov–Jan) frequently ranks among the world's most polluted — primarily due to crop burning in Punjab/Haryana + traffic + cold air trapping pollution.
🔹 Daryaganj was once the world's largest second-hand book market (Sunday market), until it moved to Mahila Haat in 2019.
🔹 The Lotus Temple receives more visitors annually than the Eiffel Tower.

🖼️ Delhi Photo Gallery

Delhi's visual richness makes it one of the world's most photogenic cities. Here's a guided visual journey:

Dawn: Jama Masjid — The first azaan echoes across Old Delhi; mist rises over the rooftops. Morning: Chandni Chowk — Milk carts, flower vendors, and rickshaws create a timeless tableau. Midday: Humayun's Tomb — The dome glows against a blue sky; charbagh geometry is perfect for architecture photography. Afternoon: Qutub Minar — The minaret casts dramatic shadows on surrounding ruins. Sunset: Lodi Garden — Golden hour turns the Tughlaq and Sayyid tombs amber; parakeets circle. Blue Hour: India Gate — The arch illuminated against deepening blue; families picnic in silhouette. Night: Hauz Khas Village — Reflections on the reservoir; lights from rooftop cafés shimmer. Annual events for photography: Republic Day parade (January), Diwali fireworks from rooftops, Holi colour celebrations, CR Park Durga Puja pandals (October).

🌤️ Weather & Pollution Updates

Delhi's climate is extreme and dramatic — one of the world's most temperature-variable capitals:

Winter (Nov–Feb): Temperatures: 4°C–20°C. Dense fog grips the city December–January, disrupting flights and trains. Cold waves from the Himalayas can drop temperatures to 1–2°C. Spring (March–April): Perfect weather, 20°C–32°C. Best time to visit. Summer (May–June): Brutal; 40°C–48°C. Hot, dry "loo" winds blow from the west. Monsoon (July–September): Humidity soars; 28°C–38°C. Heavy rains, waterlogging, and flooding in low-lying areas. Air Quality: November is the worst month — crop burning + Diwali firecrackers + cold air = AQI often 400–500+ (Hazardous). GRAP (Graded Response Action Plan) triggers construction bans and school closures. Resources: IQAir.com, SAFAR India (safar.nic.in), AQI India app for real-time monitoring. Tip: Carry N95 masks November–January. Best months: February–April and September–October.
